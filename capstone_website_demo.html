Project Overview

This capstone project systematically characterizes county-level and temporal variation in Medicare Part D formulary restrictiveness for diabetes medications from 2020 through 2025—a period marked by transformative policy changes including the Part D Senior Savings Model, the Inflation Reduction Act's insulin cap, and the 2025 benefit redesign.

Using freely available CMS public data, this project documents how plans responded to these policy shifts through changes in prior authorization requirements, tier placement, and other utilization management tools across four major diabetes medication classes.

Background

Type 2 diabetes affects approximately 24.4% of Americans aged 65 and older, representing over 14 million Medicare beneficiaries and making it one of the most prevalent chronic conditions in the Medicare population.1 Effective diabetes management requires consistent access to prescription medications, yet Medicare Part D formularies (the lists of covered drugs and their associated access restrictions) have become increasingly restrictive over time. Between 2011 and 2020, prior authorization, step therapy, and quantity limits expanded dramatically, with restricted compounds increasing from 31.9% to 44.4% across Part D plans.2 Most notably, prior authorization requirements for GLP-1s surged from approximately 5% of plans in the early 2020s to nearly 100% by 2025.3,4 These formulary restrictions create substantial barriers to medication access for millions of beneficiaries and may disproportionately affect vulnerable populations including racial and ethnic minorities, lower-income beneficiaries, and those with limited health literacy who face greater challenges navigating administrative barriers.

The period from 2020 to 2025 represents a particularly dynamic era for Part D policy, marked by multiple major reforms aimed at improving medication affordability and access. The Part D Senior Savings Model (2021-2023) allowed plans to voluntarily cap insulin copayments at $35 per month, demonstrating improved adherence in participating plans.5 The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 expanded this insulin cap program-wide beginning in January 2023 and introduced additional cost-sharing protections including a $2,000 annual out-of-pocket spending cap effective January 2025.6 However, these same policy changes fundamentally restructured Part D financial liability, increasing plans' catastrophic phase costs from 15% to 60% for brand-name drugs and creating incentives for more aggressive utilization management.

Against this policy backdrop, existing research establishes that formulary restrictions impact medication access and utilization patterns. Studies consistently demonstrate that prior authorization requirements, step therapy protocols, and higher cost-sharing tiers reduce rates of medication initiation and continuation.7 Variation in access to diabetes medications has been documented across regions, with initiation rates for newer glucose-lowering drugs varying fivefold across U.S. hospital referral regions. Additionally, substantial racial and socioeconomic disparities exist in medication access, with Black beneficiaries experiencing lower rates of SGLT2 inhibitor and GLP-1 receptor agonist initiation even after adjusting for clinical factors. The Part D Senior Savings Model evaluation demonstrated that reduced insulin cost-sharing improved medication adherence among participating beneficiaries.

Despite these dramatic policy shifts, comprehensive characterization of how formulary restrictiveness varies across counties and over time remains limited and underexplored. For example, although national trends in formulary restrictions have been documented for the 2011-2020 period, comprehensive characterization of county-level variation in formulary restrictiveness remains lacking. Preliminary evidence suggests regional disparities exist, but systematic measurement across multiple diabetes medication classes has not been conducted. Additionally, despite the dramatic formulary changes occurring during 2020-2025 (particularly the near-universal adoption of prior authorization for GLP-1 receptor agonists), temporal trends during this critical policy transition remain poorly documented. Moreover, existing approaches for measuring formulary restrictiveness vary widely across studies,8 limiting comparability and hindering development of validated metrics that can be applied consistently in future research and policy monitoring.

This project addresses these gaps by systematically characterizing county-level and temporal variation in Medicare Part D formulary restrictiveness for diabetes medications from 2020 through 2025. By documenting the extent and distribution of this variation, findings will identify markets where restrictive formularies may particularly affect vulnerable populations. The temporal analysis will reveal how plans responded to major policy changes, informing ongoing debates about balancing medication cost control with ensuring access to effective therapies. This project will establish validated measures of restrictiveness using publicly accessible data sources, providing a replicable framework for continued monitoring of Part D formulary policies.

Methods

Data Sources

  • ● CMS Part D Formulary Files (2020-2025): Drug-level coverage, tier placement, prior authorization, step therapy, quantity limits
  • ● CMS Part D Enrollment Files: Plan-level enrollment by county for weighting
  • ● Plan Characteristics Files: Plan type (MA-PD vs. PDP), service areas

Restrictiveness Score Construction

A count-based restrictiveness score (0-4 scale) is calculated for each drug-plan-year combination:

Score =

  • + 1 if Prior Authorization required
  • + 1 if Step Therapy required
  • + 1 if Quantity Limits applied
  • + 1 if Tier 4 or 5 (high cost-sharing)

Geographic Aggregation

County-level restrictiveness scores are calculated using enrollment-weighted averages across all plans serving each county. This approach reflects the actual formulary environment experienced by beneficiaries based on where they are enrolled, rather than giving equal weight to small and large plans.

Analysis Approach

Temporal Analysis

Track national trends in restrictiveness by drug class across 2020-2025, identifying inflection points around policy changes

Geographic Analysis

Map county-level variation in restrictiveness, identify high- and low-restriction markets

Prior Authorization Requirements Over Time

Key Finding: GLP-1 receptor agonist PA requirements remained stable at ~5-11% through 2023, then surged dramatically to 78% in 2024 and 95% in 2025 following the IRA benefit redesign. In contrast, insulin PA requirements decreased from 18% to 4% as the $35 copay cap reduced plans' incentives for utilization management.

Restrictiveness Score by Drug Class (2025)

Key Finding: GLP-1 receptor agonists show the highest overall restrictiveness (score 2.7/4.0), driven primarily by near-universal prior authorization. All non-insulin diabetes medication classes show elevated restrictiveness compared to insulin.
